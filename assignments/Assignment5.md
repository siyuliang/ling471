Now we are going to step back and think carefully about the tool that nearly everyone in tech uses every day: large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini.

**There is almost no required programming.** Like every assignment in this course, it is graded on **completion, not correctness**: make a genuine attempt and you receive full credit. Have fun with it.

### A study to keep in mind

Recall the [Xiao et al. (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.21890) study mentioned in the syllabus. It found that **technical expertise, not AI familiarity, was the only significant predictor of homework performance**, even when every student had the same AI tool. Students with strong foundations wrote clearer prompts, gave better context, and used AI strategically. Students without that foundation used AI reactively, often getting stuck in loops of vague prompts that led nowhere. Keep this in the back of your mind as you work. You will return to it in Part 3.

### Part 1: Have a conversation

1. Start a **fresh chat** and work through your task with the LLM. Have a real back-and-forth (a handful of messages). To get something useful, try to prompt it **well**:
   - Give context (what the code is for, what you already tried, which Python version you use).
   - **Paste the actual code** or the exact error message, if relevant.
   - Be specific about what a good answer would look like.
   - Ask **at least one follow-up question** to dig deeper, or to push back on something the LLM said.

2. **Copy the transcript (partially if conversation too long)** (or take screenshots) into your report under a heading **"Part 1: Conversation."**

3. Write a short paragraph (4 to 6 sentences): Was the response useful? What did you have to tell the LLM to get a good answer, and what did it have to *guess*? Did the follow-up question change anything?

### Part 2: Fact-check the LLM

LLMs are confident even when they are wrong. This part is about the single most important skill of an LLM user: **verifying the output yourself.**

1. From your conversation, pick **one concrete claim or one piece of code** the LLM produced.

2. **Verify it yourself.** Depending on what you picked:
   - If it is code, **run it** (or step through it in the debugger) and see if it does what the LLM claims.
   - If it is a factual claim, check it against **official documentation** (e.g., the Python docs), the course lectures, or your textbook.

3. In your report, under **"Part 2: Fact-check,"** write about 4 to 6 sentences: What did you check? Was the LLM right, partly right, or wrong? **What would you have done if you had not taken this course?**

> It is completely fine, even good, if the LLM turns out to be correct. The point is that *you checked*, and that you now know how.
